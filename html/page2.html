 Video game design must be one of the strangest fields in the world, because when magic is involved nearly all video games use the same 'creative' systems. That is largely because they work.

 Where some players feel like magic in video games has become largely unmagical, the kind of library-going ancient-text-translating wizard would be slow and cumbersome for most games. In its stead, we get two systems: magic programming and runecrafting.

 Magic programming, as its name suggests, is fairly conducive to the digital medium because it revolves around the player adjusting set points in a spell's creation. For example: Two Worlds II uses a payload, a carrier, and a small number of modifiers. In this system, a basic fire ball would be a projectile carrier with a fire payload and a damage modifier (or else the fire only blinds opponents). As the player gets more payloads, carriers, and modifiers, represented in the game as purchasable cards, they can create more creative and powerful spells that can duplicate, heat-seek, summon allies, etc. This kind of system is predictable on the developer's side and fairly easy to program without introducing bugs or imbalance. The player, in turn, is provided with a system that encourages creativity and experimentation with what cards they can obtain. This kind of system provides a middle ground for magic users who want to mess with powerful magic and wreak havoc and magic users who want to solve problems and be clever to use magic.

 Even further to the side of experimentation is the runecrafting system. Games such as the Underworld series occasionally give players magical runes with little instruction as to how they can be used. Upon discovering a new rune, the player needs to experiment by combining that rune with their other runes to see if they have new spells. Some games even require the player to keep track of their spells on their own. This system provides nothing for a power-seeking mage, but feels more academic, or even scientific, to a player who wants to feel like a pen-and-parchment wizard.
