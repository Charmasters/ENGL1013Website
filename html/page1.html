 We live in a sad universe that doesn't permit us to fire lightning or flames from our fingertips. Well, we're close, but that requires money, intellect, and access to a barren landscape where it isn't illegal yet.

 Thankfully, most fantasy worlds don't have those reasonable limitations. Without needing to find a new job, anyone can pretend to be a powerful wizard and take their frustrations out on unsuspecting townsfolk for the price of a replacement desk and drywall.

 Under Natalie Garza's categories of motivation to play games, these kinds of sandboxes provide the player a sense of autonomy, and use just enough rules to mitigate decision paralysis and ironically facilitate player choice. If these limitations were not set in place, the game would be about as cathartic as taking your frustrations on a set of unsuspecting dominoes.

 Many games that provide a power fantasy also include the rise to that power. Some, like Skyrim, force the player to engage in other aspects of the game like the story or character building (or just using a wiki). Others tempt the player into improving themselves and their skills as a wizard. They might collect better abilities over time, but the player themself grows from an average office worker into a wizard of legend.

 Therefore, the desire for power through simulated magic is better qualified by Garza's category of mastery. Games that focus on it give clear metrics for the player's carnage or the exact increase in their skills, and can rival the feeling of productivity from a brand new and lesser paying office job.

 None of this is to say that no games with magic are designed to provide the player with a sense of autonomy or personal freedom, but for the chaos craving button pusher, this system works just fine.
