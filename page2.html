 Whenever someone is not working by habit, they are being creative, whether they are looking for cheaper ways to purchase groceries or developing entirely new groceries. In her article, Karen Kersting quotes Dr. Dean Simonton in explaining the difference between daily-living little-c creativity and mastery big-C creativity (Kerstring).

 Magic systems in games can create unique challenges of survival where the player must learn how to effectively use magic and learn its worth compared to other systems. This would be most obvious in some fantasy MMOs where magic services can be sold and discovering efficiencies would be rewarded. While the decisions as to when to use magic versus to other methods would be more like little-c creativity, complex fantasy games can push players towards making big-C breakthroughs.

 However, even single player games such as Divinity: Original Sin and Noita encourage interesting styles of play. While in both the player can see basic attributes of the spells themselves, magic has extra properties the player can use.

 In Divinity, for example, the player could use fireball constantly, but if the player notices that fog conducts electricity, they utilize a seaside environment to hit more enemies for a lower cost. Similarly, Noita simulates every pixel and gives them interesting properties.

 In Noita, the player can learn and manipulate the properties of water, fire, blood, goo, and more to their advantage. Furthermore, the player can modify wands with different spells and components. Unfortunately, where these kinds of games would want to encourage players to experiment and have breakthroughs, the harshness of Noita discourages players from doing so.

 Still, a creative magic system can teach people how to be creative in unfamiliar situations in ways that rival dedicated strategy or puzzle games. Where those distill the tactical options and place them in front of you, magic can wait for the player to think of new ways of using it.
